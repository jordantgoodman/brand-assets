Why Arial for DOCX — The Font Substitution Problem

Every font has unique character metrics — the exact width of each letter, the height of ascenders and descenders, the spacing between characters. When Word can't find the specified font, it substitutes the closest system font — but the metrics never match exactly. Here's what actually breaks:

Designing in Arial (What You Send = What They See)

You design the document in Arial. You see exactly how it looks. Line breaks, page breaks, table column widths, header spacing — all verified. The recipient opens it on any machine and sees the identical layout, because Arial is on every computer ever made. No surprises.

Designing in Brand Font (What You Send ≠ What They See)

You design in Plus Jakarta Sans. It looks perfect on your machine. The recipient opens it on Windows — they don't have Plus Jakarta Sans, so Word silently substitutes Calibri. Calibri has different character widths, so: lines break in different places, tables overflow or leave awkward gaps, page breaks shift (content moves to wrong pages), carefully positioned headers land in the wrong spot. The recipient has no idea it's supposed to look different — they just see a messy document.

Can you predict the substitution and design around it? Not reliably. Word's substitution algorithm considers the font's PANOSE classification, family type, and metrics — and different versions of Word on different operating systems may choose different substitutes. A document that substitutes to Calibri on Windows 11 might substitute to something else on Windows 10 or on a government machine with restricted fonts. You'd be designing for a moving target.

The rule: Brand font for HTML (loads from web) and PDF (font embeds in file). Arial for DOCX (universal rendering). This isn't a compromise — it's the only way to guarantee every recipient sees a professional document.
